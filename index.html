Research

Over the past several months, I have worked as an undergraduate research assistant in experimental nuclear physics under the supervision of Dr. Nadia Fomin at the University of Tennessee. I work closely with a PhD student on the reassembly, characterization, and operation of a dynamic nuclear polarization (DNP) characterization apparatus transferred from Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL). The long-term goal of this project is to optimize a polarized target system for future photon scattering experiments in Hall D at the Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility (JLab).

This project has provided me with a high degree of independence whilst working within a collaborative research team. My responsibilities included literature review, computational work, hands-on experimental work, and technical presentations, ranging from weekly progress meetings with collaborators to larger venues such as the APS Division of Nuclear Physics annual conference. Computationally, I have become highly proficient in Python through analyzing how nuclear polarization is measured using a Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) coil within a DNP system. I performed a three-Gaussian fitting analysis on some provided ammonia polarization data from Jefferson Lab’s Run Group C (2022–2023), generating time dependent plots of the Gaussian amplitudes, centers, and widths. Once a week I presented these results to Dr. Fomin and collaborators at JLab and ORNL, contributing to ongoing discussions to investigate the physical meaning behind a misunderstood background signal contributing to the system’s overall polarization.

In the laboratory, I have gained extensive experience working with the cryogenic systems of our DNP target setup. Our setup uses a BlueFors dry dilution refrigerator, which I helped reassemble and bring back into operating condition. Working closely with the PhD student, we have successfully completed two cooldowns, reaching temperatures just below 30 mK. Our analysis of these cooldowns revealed a heat leak within the system, and I am actively involved in locating and eliminating its source. Once sustained operation below 20 mK is achieved, I will apply controlled heat loads to map the refrigerator’s power curve and create a “heat budget” to guide the design of future experiments.
